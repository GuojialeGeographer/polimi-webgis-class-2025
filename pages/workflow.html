Project Workflow

The methodology and steps followed in our air quality analysis

1. Data Collection

The first step in our analysis involved collecting various datasets relevant to air quality across Greece:

  • Air Quality Monitoring Data: Historical and real-time measurements from monitoring stations across Greece, including concentrations of PM2.5, PM10, NO2, O3, and other pollutants.
  • Meteorological Data: Weather conditions that influence air pollution dispersion, including wind speed/direction, temperature, precipitation, and atmospheric pressure from Greece's varied climate zones.
  • Land Use Data: Information on urban areas, industrial zones, agricultural regions, islands, green spaces, and transportation networks that affect pollution sources and patterns.
  • Traffic Data: Traffic volume and flow patterns on major roads and highways, with special attention to tourist areas during peak seasons.
  • Population Data: Demographic information to assess population exposure to air pollution and identify vulnerable groups across different regions.
  • Shipping Data: Maritime traffic information for major Greek ports and shipping lanes in the Aegean and Ionian seas.
  • Wildfire Data: Historical wildfire events and their impact on seasonal air quality patterns.

2. Data Processing and Preparation

Raw data underwent several processing steps to prepare it for analysis:

  • Cleaning and validation to identify and handle missing values, outliers, and inconsistencies
  • Temporal aggregation to create daily, monthly, and annual averages
  • Spatial interpolation to generate continuous pollution surfaces from point measurements across Greece's diverse terrain
  • Coordinate system transformations and geographic data standardization
  • Integration of diverse datasets into a cohesive geospatial database
  • Regional stratification to account for Greece's mainland, peninsular, and island territories

3. Spatial Analysis

We employed various spatial analysis techniques to understand the distribution and determinants of air pollution:

  • Kriging Interpolation: Created continuous pollution surfaces from discrete monitoring points, with topographic adjustments for Greece's mountainous regions
  • Hotspot Analysis: Identified statistically significant clusters of high pollution values in urban centers, industrial zones, and seasonal tourist destinations
  • Buffer Analysis: Examined pollution levels in proximity to major roads, ports, industrial sites, and protected natural areas
  • Overlay Analysis: Combined pollution data with land use and population layers to assess exposure across different regions
  • Temporal Trend Analysis: Evaluated changes in pollution patterns over different time periods, with special attention to seasonal tourist impacts
  • Coastal-Inland Comparisons: Analyzed differences between coastal zones and inland regions

4. Air Quality Index Calculation

We calculated the Air Quality Index (AQI) following standard methodologies:

  • Sub-indices were computed for each pollutant (PM2.5, PM10, NO2, O3, etc.)
  • The overall AQI was determined by the highest sub-index value
  • AQI values were classified into six categories: Good, Moderate, Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups, Unhealthy, Very Unhealthy, and Hazardous
  • Daily AQI maps were generated for the entire country and then aggregated to create seasonal and annual representations
  • Regional AQI comparisons highlighted differences between urban centers, island communities, and rural areas

5. Health Risk Assessment

To evaluate potential health impacts, we conducted a risk assessment that considered:

  • Population exposure to different levels of air pollution across Greece's diverse regions
  • Identification of vulnerable demographic groups (children, elderly, those with pre-existing conditions)
  • Correlation of pollution levels with health data from Greek health authorities
  • Creation of a health risk index that combines pollution intensity with population vulnerability
  • Special consideration for seasonal population changes due to tourism in coastal and island areas

6. WebGIS Implementation

Finally, we developed an interactive WebGIS platform to visualize and share our findings:

  • GeoServer was used to publish spatial data as Web Map Services (WMS)
  • OpenLayers provided the foundation for the interactive web mapping application
  • Various base maps and overlay layers were configured for exploring Greece's air quality patterns
  • Interactive features such as layer toggling, feature identification, and legends were implemented
  • The system was designed to be user-friendly for both technical and non-technical users
  • Special tools were developed for comparing mainland, coastal, and island air quality patterns

Data Management

Our project utilized PostgreSQL/PostGIS for storing and managing geospatial data, enabling efficient querying and analysis of complex spatial relationships among air quality factors across Greece's diverse landscapes.
